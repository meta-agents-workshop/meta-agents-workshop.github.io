Proposal

Agents Managing Agents

Workshop on Responsible Use of Meta-Agents

NeurIPS 2026 · December 11-12, 2026 · Sydney, Australia

Agents that design, optimize, train, and oversee other agents.

About The Workshop

Recent jumps in agent capability show early signs of recursive self-improvement. AlphaEvolve evolved and tested its own candidate programs and found faster algorithms. GEPA and Meta-Harness reflected on the traces of a whole language-model program to optimize it. The Darwin Gödel Machine and HyperAgents rewrote their own code and improved on a real software-engineering benchmark with no human edits. In each case the worker was an agent acting on another agent.

We call these higher-order agents meta-agents: agents that build, train, optimize, or improve other agents, sometimes themselves. Most agents today are still assembled by hand. A meta-agent removes that limit, letting a model design, train, or optimize another model and search a space no human team covers by hand. Two problems stay open: when does a discovered or self-improved design actually generalize, and when a system improves, which change earned the credit?

Capability forces a second question. When the thing being changed is itself an agent, a flawed design gets copied into every agent the search builds, and a system that edits itself can edit away its own safeguards. So every result raises the same question: who evaluates and oversees an agent that builds or improves another agent, or itself? We pair strong capability work with honest accounts of how methods fail, and we bring in oversight, governance, and management-science views from outside AI. The workshop builds on the team's 1st Multi-Turn Interaction Workshop @ NeurIPS 2025.



Topics

We invite work across the topics below, capability first, then evaluation, oversight, and governance. Topics include but are not limited to:

(1) Automated Design and Discovery of Agent Harnesses:

Most agent harnesses are still built by hand. We invite work on searching the space of agent architectures, tools, and harnesses (Meta Agent Search, automated harness design), and on telling when a discovered design generalizes past the tasks it was tuned on.

(2) Optimizing Compound AI Systems and Language-Model Programs:

Once an agent is more than one model call, the problem is optimizing the whole program. We seek work on optimizers for multi-module systems (DSPy, GEPA, Trace), credit assignment across modules, inference-time search, and context optimization, including workflows and multi-agent systems.

(3) Self-Improvement and Open-Ended Evolution:

A meta-agent can point at itself. We welcome work on agents that rewrite their own code (the Darwin Gödel Machine, self-taught optimizers), self-play and co-evolution, open-endedness, and honest analyses of how self-improving agents drift or game their own reward.

(4) Evaluation and Benchmarks for Meta-Agent Capabilities:

Does a discovered or self-improved agent actually generalize? We want benchmarks and tests for meta-level methods, the ones that answer this question rather than leaderboards that hide the failure cases.

(5) Human Oversight, Governance, and Management Science:

Rollback and halt controls a deployer can actually trigger, who signs off before a discovered agent ships, and how to govern agents that manage other agents, including perspectives from management science and other fields outside AI.

(6) Red-Teaming and Safe Meta-Agents:

Making agent execution observable and auditable, red-teaming systems that act on other agents, and detecting reward gaming, over-optimization, and drift before a system acts on other agents at scale.



Call For Papers

Agents Managing Agents (NeurIPS 2026) invites submissions on architectures, algorithms, theory, empirical studies, benchmarks, and position papers about agents that design, optimize, supervise, train, or improve other agents. Submissions must present original, unpublished work that has not appeared at NeurIPS or other archival machine-learning venues.

Key Dates

  • Submission Deadline: August 29, 2026, AoE (tentative)
  • Notification: on or before September 29, 2026, AoE
  • Workshop Date: December 11-12, 2026 (Sydney)
All deadlines follow the Anywhere on Earth (AoE) timezone. Final dates will be confirmed upon workshop acceptance and aligned with the NeurIPS 2026 timeline.

Submission Site

Submissions will be managed via OpenReview and remain private during review. All authors should maintain up-to-date OpenReview profiles for conflict-of-interest management and paper matching. The submission portal will be announced once the workshop is accepted.

Scope

We welcome contributions across the topics above. Accepted papers are presented as posters, with a subset selected for oral or spotlight talks, and we give a Best Social Impact award to the work that best surfaces and contains the risks of acting on other agents, judged apart from raw capability. The workshop is in person at NeurIPS 2026 in Sydney.

Submission Guidelines

Formatting
Submissions must be in English and use the NeurIPS 2026 workshop LaTeX template. Papers are submitted as a single PDF:
  • Full Papers: at most 8 pages (main text)
  • Short Papers: at most 4 pages (main text)
  • Tiny Papers: encouraged from students and under-resourced groups, welcoming simple ideas, modest results, follow-ups, or fresh perspectives.
  • Demo Track: live demonstrations of meta-agent systems and tools, presented alongside the poster sessions.
  • Position Papers: on the governance, oversight, and societal impact of meta-agents.
  • References and appendices do not count toward the page limit, but the main text must be self-contained.
Meta-Agent Card

Every submission includes a one-page Meta-Agent Card: what the method optimizes and against which signal, the scope of change it makes to the agent it acts on, how a deployer would test that the result generalizes, the failure modes specific to acting on other agents (reward gaming, drift, brittle self-edits, runaway search), and the guardrails or rollback that catch them. The card is reviewed alongside the technical contribution, and we run a pre-deadline clinic to help newer and under-resourced groups complete it.

Responsible-Use Statement

Every submission also carries a short responsible-use statement: who could be harmed if the method is wrong or misused, and what would catch it. Like the Meta-Agent Card, it is reviewed with the paper, and a missing one is grounds for desk rejection.

Anonymity
The workshop uses double-blind review. Submissions must be anonymized, with author names, affiliations, and acknowledgments removed and prior work cited in the third person.

Non-Archival Policy

The workshop is non-archival. Papers under review elsewhere are welcome, and accepted papers may be published at other venues afterward. Work already published at NeurIPS or other archival ML venues should not be submitted.

Speakers

Invited speakers (eight talks; several confirmed, remaining invitations in progress). Talk topics below are illustrative and will be finalized with each speaker.


Omar Khattab

MIT

Talk: Compiling, Optimizing, and Evaluating Compound AI Systems
Treating an agent as a program that compiles against a metric (DSPy, GEPA), and the evaluation needed to keep an optimizer improving the system rather than gaming the metric.


Chelsea Finn

Stanford University

Talk: From Meta-Learning to Agents that Learn to Learn
How learning-to-learn, from MAML to the meta-harness, carries into agents that tune their own scaffolding, and where human oversight has to sit.


Yu Su

NeoCognition & Ohio State University

Talk: Continual Learning and Memory for Agents
Agents that keep learning as their tasks, tools, and sub-agents change, and how to keep that adaptation reliable and inspectable.


Graham Neubig

Carnegie Mellon University

Talk: Automating and Evaluating the Agent Harness
Automating the software-engineering agent harness (OpenHands) and the recursive optimization of agents, with the evaluation needed to trust the result.


Jenny Zhang

Recursive & UBC

Talk: Self-Improving Agents: Open-Ended Discovery and Its Failure Modes
The Darwin Godel Machine and HyperAgents as open-ended self-improvement, and the failure modes that appear when an agent rewrites its own code and its successors.


Chen Sun

Google DeepMind

Talk: World Models and Learned Environments for Agents
Learned world models and environments as the substrate a meta-agent uses to train, simulate, and evaluate other agents.


Robert Lange

Sakana AI

Talk: Nature-Inspired and Evolutionary Methods for Self-Improving Agents
Evolutionary, nature-inspired methods (The AI Scientist, ShinkaEvolve) for agents that improve other agents, and how to keep open-ended search auditable.


Bo Li

University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign

Talk: Red-Teaming and Safety Evaluation for Meta-Agents
Trustworthy-ML tools for agents that act on other agents: red-teaming, robustness, and safety benchmarks for the meta level.
